Security Thinking in Practice

Behavioral Risk · Incident Response

Identifying Coordinated Abuse in a Live System

Noticed multiple accounts using different emails but identical shipping addresses, followed by repeated low-value orders. Analyzed the pattern, identified it as likely coordinated behavior, and implemented platform controls to restrict repeat address reuse. Monitored whether activity continued post-change.

This shifted how I think — away from individual transactions and toward system-wide behavior and intent. Pattern recognition as a security discipline, not just an operational one.

Threat Thinking · Anomaly Detection

Distinguishing Legitimate Users from Coordinated Activity

Observed non-standard purchasing behavior across multiple accounts. Compared behavioral signals — timing, frequency, address overlap — to determine whether this was isolated or systemic. Identified repeated patterns consistent with automated or coordinated activity.

I started thinking less like a store owner and more like a systems analyst — focused on patterns, intent, and behavior at scale rather than individual events.

GRC · OSINT Thinking · Digital Footprint Risk

Compliance, Labeling, and External Exposure Awareness

From early on in building my brand, I paid attention to how product descriptions, labeling language, and online presentation could carry compliance and perception risk for personal care products. When an external PR message surfaced broader trends in clean skincare labeling, I used it as a prompt to scan my own website and digital presence — reviewing wording, consistency, and potential areas of ambiguity across all public-facing content.

This led to ongoing refinements in how products were described and how the brand appeared externally, building a more structured approach to reviewing public-facing information for risk exposure.

Risk isn't only internal. External perception, language, and digital footprint carry their own exposure — and monitoring them requires the same rigor as internal controls. This is where my GRC and OSINT thinking began.

How I Think

The STARR Method — Structured by design, not by habit

Every problem I work through — whether a customer escalation at Apple or a security scenario in my business — follows this same framework. The final R is the one most skip: without Reflection, you're set up to solve the same problem twice.

S
Situation
Understand the full context before drawing conclusions
T
Task
Define the specific responsibility or problem to solve
A
Action
Take structured, intentional steps toward resolution
R
Result
Document the outcome and its measurable impact
R
Reflection
Close the loop — what was learned, what changes next time
